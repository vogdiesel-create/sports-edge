### The most important finding: CLV is everything.

When we beat the closing line, we win 77% of the time. When we don't, we win 45%. That 32pp spread means CLV isn't just a tracking metric - it's the SIGNAL. If I can build a pre-game CLV estimator, the model becomes dramatically more selective and profitable.

Features I think predict +CLV:
- Model edge size (higher edge -> more likely to beat close)
- Poisson/XGBoost agreement (when both agree, more likely to be right)
- Line age (older lines = more stale = easier to beat)
- Weather factor (weather models improve closer to game time, early bets capture stale weather)

### The K-prop bias is fixable.

+0.58 K overestimation is systematic. Two approaches:
1. Simple: subtract 0.58 from expected Ks. Fast, easy, might be too crude.
2. Better: regress the offset by pitcher type (elite K-rate pitchers might have different bias than low-K pitchers). More work but more accurate.

### What worries me.

- 68 bets is still small. Could be running hot.
- CLV is negative on average (-0.42%). We're behind the market more than ahead of it.
- We haven't faced a real drawdown yet. The worst day was -$336 (Apr 17) but we recovered immediately. A 5-day cold streak would test the Kelly sizing.

## Research Directions for Next Week

1. **CLV prediction** - highest ROI research. Even a crude filter (model agreement + edge size) could cut noise bets.
2. **K-prop bias correction** - quick win. Subtract 0.58 and backtest.
3. **Stuff+ integration** - PitchingBot and Stuff+ metrics could replace K/9 as the core K-prop input. FanGraphs has this data.
4. **Conway-Maxwell-Poisson** - recent paper shows better fit than negative binomial for baseball scoring. Worth investigating.
5. **Bullpen quality** - our model ignores bullpen entirely. For totals, the last 3-4 innings are bullpen, and bullpen fatigue/quality varies massively.
