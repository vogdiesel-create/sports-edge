# Source Credibility Audit: Sports Betting YouTube Content

**Agent**: web-researcher
**Domain**: Source credibility analysis, claim verification
**Date**: 2026-04-20

---

## Executive Summary

Most sources reviewed are **legitimate professionals** with real track records, not pure grifters. However, they ALL monetize their knowledge (through platforms, courses, or media deals), creating inherent bias toward making betting seem more accessible than it is. The specific claims we incorporated range from VERIFIED (academic/statistical) to UNVERIFIED (specific numeric parameters with no traceable source).

## PART 2: CLAIM VERIFICATION

---

### Claim 1: "Putaway rate vs whiff rate divergence is the primary bet trigger for K props"

**Source attribution**: Koerner (Payoff Pitch)

**Verification**:
- Putaway rate is a real, defined Statcast metric (MLB.com glossary confirmed)
- Whiff rate (SwStr%) is a foundational K predictor (extensively verified)
- The DIVERGENCE between them as a "primary bet trigger" -- I could NOT find this specific claim articulated anywhere outside our notes
- Koerner discusses K props on Payoff Pitch but the specific "divergence = primary trigger" framing may be our interpretation of his broader methodology
- Multiple betting sites discuss using BOTH metrics but frame them as complementary inputs, not specifically as a divergence signal

**Rating: PLAUSIBLE** -- Both metrics are real and relevant. The "divergence" framing as the PRIMARY trigger appears to be either our synthesis or a specific Koerner claim from a podcast that isn't documented in text anywhere. The underlying logic is sound but the specific articulation may be oversimplified.

---

### Claim 2: "Full Kelly leads to bankruptcy in 100% of realistic simulations"

**Source attribution**: Wharton paper

**Verification**: VERIFIED
- Wharton Student Research Journal (Spring 2023, Beggy) explicitly states: "Full Kelly scenarios did not work in a realistic betting environment; it led to bankruptcy in 100% of the scenarios."
- Paper recommends partial Kelly with coefficient 0.50
- Edward Thorp's simulations also show full Kelly's extreme downside risk
- Berkeley paper (MacLean) confirms "bad properties" of full Kelly
- Multiple academic sources corroborate

**Rating: VERIFIED** -- Real Wharton paper, real finding. Note: this is a STUDENT research paper (not faculty), but the methodology and finding are consistent with broader academic literature on Kelly criterion.

---

### Claim 3: "Two-way props hold ~4% vs multi-way at ~14%"

**Source attribution**: Captain Jack

**Verification**:
- Two-way markets: Standard -110/-110 = 4.5% hold (VERIFIED)
- Player props (two-way over/under): 8-15% hold typical (higher than claimed 4%)
- Multi-way markets/futures: 12-25% hold (14% is within range)
- The claim as stated is PARTIALLY WRONG: two-way SPREADS hold ~4%, but two-way PROPS typically hold 8-15%

**Rating: PLAUSIBLE with caveat** -- The ranges are real, but conflates "two-way spreads" (4%) with "two-way props" (8-15%). If Jack specifically said "two-way props hold ~4%," that understates the typical prop vig. More likely he was comparing prop over/unders (~8-10% on soft books) vs multi-way futures (~14-25%).

---

### Claim 4: "No book is truly sharp on props"

**Source attribution**: Spanky/Unabated

**Verification**: VERIFIED by multiple sources
- Establish The Run article: "Understanding the Current Ecosystem of NFL Player Props" confirms props are softer
- Pinnacle and Bookmaker hang LOW limits on props ($250-500), indicating they don't trust their own lines
- Wizard of Odds article on props confirms mathematical inefficiencies
- Multiple sources confirm: sharp money concentrates on sides/totals where limits are $10K+, leaving props under-policed
- The Gambling Site article explicitly: "Sportsbooks hate prop bets" because they know their lines are soft
- Sportsbook Scout/Pikkit confirm: "no sharpest sportsbook for props" -- even Pinnacle caps at $250

**Rating: VERIFIED** -- This is industry consensus. The mechanism is clear: low limits = less sharp action = softer lines. This is one of the most well-established edges in modern sports betting.

---

### Claim 5: "MLB_OVERDISPERSION should be 2.1-2.2"

**Source attribution**: FanGraphs community research

**Verification**:
- MLB data IS overdispersed relative to Poisson (VERIFIED concept)
- Negative binomial distribution is recommended for MLB modeling (VERIFIED)
- The SPECIFIC parameter value of 2.1-2.2 for strikeout overdispersion: NOT FOUND in any source
- FanGraphs community blog discusses negative binomial for RUNS, not specifically strikeouts
- DRatings has an "MLB Strikeout Distribution Analysis" page but returned 403 (couldn't access)
- No academic or community source verified this specific numeric range

**Rating: UNVERIFIED** -- The concept of overdispersion in MLB is real. Using negative binomial is correct methodology. But the specific 2.1-2.2 parameter value cannot be traced to any FanGraphs community post or published research. This may have been fabricated by a YouTube source, or it's buried in a source we can't access.

---

### Claim 6: "SwStr% correlates with K% at R=0.81"

**Source attribution**: Podhorzer/FanGraphs

**Verification**: VERIFIED
- FanGraphs article "Adventures in Swinging Strike Rate vs. K Rate" confirms R=0.81 correlation
- Mike Podhorzer (RotoGraphs/FanGraphs contributor) developed the "Definitive Pitcher Expected K% Formula" building on this relationship
- Blake Murphy's "Sabermetric Mining" article also discusses this correlation
- League SwStr% (~10.7%) being roughly half K% (~22.3%) is a well-known rule of thumb
- Baseball Prospectus article "Ahead in the Count" confirms predictive relationship

**Rating: VERIFIED** -- Well-established sabermetric finding with multiple independent confirmations. The R=0.81 figure is real and traceable to FanGraphs research.

**Source attribution**: Various

**Verification**:
- CONFIRMED: In 2009, Peabody made his $25K annual salary equivalent in his FIRST MONTH of MLB betting
- At that pace, a full season would be $150K+ (6 months)

- However, the SPECIFIC "$70K in a single MLB season" figure was not found in any interview or article
- What IS documented: He's bet $1 billion on golf (Fox Sports), Massey-Peabody has 56%+ lifetime ATS

**Rating: PLAUSIBLE** -- Entirely consistent with his documented abilities but the specific $70K figure isn't traceable. He likely said something like this on a podcast that wasn't transcribed. Given his verified first-month performance ($25K), $70K over a season is actually modest for his level.

---

### Claim 8: "April-May is the highest-edge window for player props"

**Source attribution**: General betting wisdom from multiple sources

**Verification**:
- PARTIALLY SUPPORTED -- Multiple sources confirm early season = more variance + less stable lines
- ATSWins article on "MLB April Betting Trends" confirms: "Small samples inflate variance like crazy... during the first two weeks, literally anything can happen"
- However, the NUANCE matters: sources actually recommend being MORE CAUTIOUS on props in April ("lean on priors... don't go heavy until you've seen a guy's pitch mix stabilize")
- The edge exists in THEORY (books have less data to price off), but so does YOUR model's uncertainty
- Zerillo's model shows March/April as his BEST period (+20.11 units) but also acknowledges it's when books are weakest
- Counterpoint: if your model is also less certain, the "edge" may be illusory

**Rating: PLAUSIBLE with major caveat** -- Books ARE less sharp early season, but so are YOUR projections. The claim is only true if your model uses pre-season information (spring training, prior year data, Statcast) better than books do. It's NOT automatically an edge just because it's April.

---

### Claim 9: "Pinnacle closing line is the gold standard for true probability"

**Source attribution**: Industry consensus

**Verification**: VERIFIED
- R-squared = 0.997 between Pinnacle closing lines and observed outcomes (397,935 football games)
- Joseph Buchdahl (respected researcher) has published extensively confirming CLV as the best predictor of long-term profitability
- Removing Pinnacle's margin shows only ~1% theoretical loss
- Football Trading Academy, JediBets, Trademate Sports, and virtually every serious betting resource confirms this
- Pinnacle has operated since 1998, welcomes sharp action, and uses closing lines that incorporate maximum information

**Rating: VERIFIED** -- This is the single most well-established fact in sports betting analytics. If anything in this list is "VERIFIED," it's this one.

---

### Claim 10: "Times-through-the-order K rate drops significantly"

**Source attribution**: Koerner, Captain Jack

**Verification**: VERIFIED
- The "Third Time Through the Order Penalty" (TTOP) is extensively documented
- OPS+ goes from 91 to 117 third time through
- ERA jumps from 4.08 to 4.57
- By 2024, MLB starters averaged only 5.24 innings on ~86 pitches (teams already act on this)
- Multiple academic papers, FanGraphs articles, and industry sources confirm
- This directly affects K props: a pitcher's K rate in innings 1-3 vs innings 5-6 is materially different

**Rating: VERIFIED** -- One of the most well-documented phenomena in baseball analytics. Directly relevant to K prop modeling.

### Critical Findings

1. **The MLB_OVERDISPERSION parameter (2.1-2.2) is the most concerning claim** -- we cannot trace it to any source. This should be empirically validated against actual strikeout distributions before trusting it.

2. **The "April-May edge" needs nuance** -- it's not a free edge. It's only exploitable if our model has better priors than the books, which requires proving.

3. **The prop market inefficiency thesis IS real** -- this is our strongest validated foundation. Multiple independent sources confirm books are structurally weak on props.

4. **Everyone is selling something** -- even the most legitimate sources (Peabody, Jack Andrews) monetize through platforms. Their incentive is to make betting seem learnable (which drives platform signups), even though fewer than 3% of bettors are profitable long-term.
